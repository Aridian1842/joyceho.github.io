Research Projects

High-throughput Phenotyping on Electronic Health Records using Multi-Tensor Factorization

Our research addresses the problem of transforming raw EHR data to medical concepts with minimal human intervention. We posit the use of multi-relational tensor factorization approaches to generate concise and clinically relevant phenotypes. The proposed computational framework will provide powerful, data-driven, and interpretable approaches for transforming high-dimensional EHR data into medical concepts.

This work is done in collaboration with Georgia Institute of Technology, Vanderbilt University, and Northwestern University.

Medication usage amongst chronic fatigue syndrome patients

Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (CFS) is a complex and devastating illness of unknown cause and origin. Managing CFS can be as complex as the illness itself: patients are often on multiple medications to manage the symptoms. It has presented a challenge to use conventional analytical approaches in examining the patterns and impacts of these medication uses. We posit that tensor factorization can be used to improve our understanding of CFS as they provide a natural framework for flexible representation and analysis of multi-aspect data.

The project started as summer internship and is an ongoing collaboration with the CFS group. My collaborators at the CDC are Jin-Mann (Sally) Lin and Brian M. Gurbaxani.

High-throughput phenotyping via tensor factorization

Electronic health records (EHR) based phenotyping is the process of mapping EHR data to meaningful medical concepts, where each concept contains a set of clinical features. Current phenotyping approaches require labor intensive supervision from experienced clinical professionals. We proposed a nonnegative tensor factorization method to derive phenotype definitions with virtually no human supervision, representing the data source interactions naturally using tensors (a generalization of matrices). In particular, we investigate the interaction of diagnoses and medications among patients. The resulting tensor factors are reported as phenotype candidates that automatically reveal patient clusters on specific diagnoses and medications. Using the proposed method, multiple phenotypes can be identified simultaneously from data.

This work was performed during my summer internship in the Healthcare Analytics group, mentored by Jimeng Sun.

DYNACARE: Dynamic cardiac arrest risk estimation

A semi-supervised time-series framework to predict the cardiac arrest time for high-risk ICU patients. We represent a patient's cardiac arrest risk trajectory (CAT) using a single latent (hidden) process. A cardiac arrest event is defined as two temporal patterns in CAT: (i) a gradient-based signature, where we assumed two heterogeneous dynamics of the risk factor (a healthy and risky state) governed the differences in the clinical measurements; or (ii) a locality-based signature based on the threshold model commonly used in toxicology to encapsulate the idea that the patient is "safe" provided the CAT is below a specific value.

This work is a collaboration with Yubin Park and Carlos Carvalho.

Multiple Sclerosis Risk Prediction Model

Multiple sclerosis (MS) is a chronic, disabling autoimmune disease that affects the central nervous system. Early diagnosis and treatment of the disease have been shown to be effective at slowing the development of disabilities. However, early MS diagnosis is difficult because symptoms are intermittent and shared with other diseases. We investigated the use of commonly available data in electronic medical records (EMRs) to create a risk prediction model; thereby helping clinicians perform the difficult task of diagnosing an MS patient.

This work was performed during my summer internship in NorthShore Clinical Research Center, mentored by KP Unnikrishnan.

Septic shock prediction for patients with partial observations

Sepsis is one of the leading causes of mortality in intensive care unit (ICU) patients. However, development of highly accurate predictive models for medical applications is often complicated by the nature of clinical data, which are typically noisy and inconsistently gathered. A commonly used solution to avoid missing data is to simply ignore subjects or features that have missing data, which can cause dramatic decreases in sample sizes or feature spaces. Our work investigates the role and impact of imputation methods while building predictive models for septic shock. By limiting our features to commonly observed, mostly non-invasive clinical measurements, we build better predictive models that are generalizable to broader groups of ICU patients.

This work was performed in collaboration with Cheng H. Lee.

PhysioNet / Computing in Cardiology 2012 Challenge

The focus was to develop methods for patient-specific prediction of in-hospital mortality. We used 42 variables (37 time-varying measurements) collected during the first two days of an ICU stay to predict which patients survive their hospitalizations, and which patients do not. Our entry placed in the top 10 (of 23) for both evaluation categories.

My teammates included Cheng H. Lee, Natalia M Arzeno, and Haris Vikalo.
